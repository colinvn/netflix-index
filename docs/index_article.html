Implicit Price Discrimination: The Netflix Index

Netflix is cheaper in poorer countries — but cheap enough?

Netflix prices vary widely by country. A standard subscription costs around $29 in Switzerland, about $7 in India, and as little as $2.80 in Pakistan. At first glance, this looks almost fair: wealthier countries pay more, poorer countries pay less. But is that actually true once you account for local purchasing power?

This project builds a Netflix index — a relative price measure that sets subscription costs in relation to each country's GDP per capita. The result partly flips the original picture upside down, and sheds light on what economists call third-degree price discrimination: firms segmenting markets and charging different prices in different segments.

All code is available on GitHub. The full repository is available on Github.

Absolute vs. relative prices

Price discrimination is not inherently unfair. If a firm charges more in wealthier markets and less in poorer ones, that may simply reflect local purchasing power — a practice sometimes called fair price differentiation. The question is whether Netflix's regional pricing actually achieves this.

Previous analyses have mostly focused on the absolute prices: how many US dollars a subscription costs in each country. Those plots confirm the intuition that richer countries pay more. But if we also account for differences in living standards, the picture looks quite different.

To make this tangible, consider the two maps below. Both show the monthly price of the Standard plan across the world, but from two different perspectives:

Netflix price per country (absolute costs in US$ per month, Standard plan) Netflix index per country (relative costs, min–max normalised)

Placed side by side, these two maps are the visual core of the analysis. In the first, the Global North dominates: Switzerland, Liechtenstein, and the US are visibly expensive. In the second, the colouring inverts: Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and parts of Southeast Asia are now the darkest areas, while Monaco and other high-income territories sit at the low end of the index.

Observation (absolute): Countries in the Global North pay more for their Netflix subscriptions.

Observation (relative): The price differences in the absolute view fail to even out the difference in purchasing power. Once we factor in GDP per capita, the relative costs in the Global South are in fact much higher.

The rest of the article explains how this index is constructed.

Data pipeline overview

The analysis combines three main data sources and proceeds in six steps:

  1. Scrape Netflix prices — get country-level Standard-plan prices from Netflix, in local currencies.
  2. Convert prices to USD — clean the price strings, map currency notations, and apply ECB/XE exchange rates.
  3. Retrieve GDP per capita — pull the latest GDP per capita in US dollars from the World Bank.
  4. Compute relative Netflix prices — divide the USD Netflix price by GDP per capita for each country.
  5. Create the Netflix index — log-transform the relative prices, remove extreme outliers, and min–max scale to a 0–100 index.
  6. Visualise the results — build static choropleth maps and an interactive Folium map with a layer toggle.

The remainder of the article follows this structure.

Step 1 — Scraping Netflix prices

Netflix maintains a help page that lists all country-specific plan pages. The analysis first retrieves the list of countries and their URL slugs, then loops over each country's page to extract the Standard-plan price.

import requests
from bs4 import BeautifulSoup

url_netflix = "https://help.netflix.com/node/24926"
html_netflix = requests.get(url_netflix).text
soup_netflix = BeautifulSoup(html_netflix, "lxml")

The country list is embedded in the page as a JavaScript object. After extracting it, a dictionary maps each country name to its URL slug. For each country, the code requests the localised pricing page and extracts the Standard plan price:

def extract_raw_price(country):
    """Scrape the Standard plan price for a given country."""
    link = url_netflix + countries_dict[country][0]
    soup = BeautifulSoup(requests.get(link).text, "lxml")

    # pricing section (structure differs slightly across localised pages)
    soup_pricing = soup.find("h3", string=lambda x: x and x.text.startswith("Pricing"))
    try:
        soup_parent = soup_pricing.find_parent()
    except AttributeError:
        return None  # Netflix not available in this country

    # two HTML structures exist across country pages
    snippets = soup_parent.select("div ul li") or soup_parent.select("section ul li")

    snippet_price = [s.text for s in snippets if s.text.startswith("Standard ")][0]
    # e.g. "Standard 14.99/month ..." -> "14.99"
    return snippet_price.split("Standard ")[1].split("\n")[0]

Out of 249 countries listed, 245 return a Standard-plan price. The four without Netflix availability at the time of scraping are China, North Korea, Russia, and Syria.

The raw prices look like 7.99, Rs 800, ARS14,999, 231,000 VND — a mix of currency symbols, codes, and decimal conventions.

Step 2 — Converting prices to USD

To compare prices across countries, all local Netflix prices are converted to US dollars. This requires two substeps:

  1. Clean and parse the raw price strings.
  2. Map Netflix's currency notations to ISO codes and apply exchange rates.

The cleaning function handles thousands separators, decimal commas, and currency symbols:

import re


def clean_prices(raw_price):
    """Extract numerical value and currency notation from a raw price string."""
    if pd.isna(raw_price):
        return None, None

    # currencies with comma as decimal delimiter
    currencies_with_comma_decimal = ["CHF", "R", "Kč"]
    if any(c in raw_price for c in currencies_with_comma_decimal):
        raw_price = raw_price.replace(".", "").replace(",", ".")

    pattern_price = re.compile(r"[^0-9.]")
    clean_price = float(pattern_price.sub("", raw_price))

    pattern_currency = re.compile(r"[0-9.,\s]")
    currency = pattern_currency.sub("", raw_price)

    return clean_price, currency


df_netflix[["clean_price", "currency"]] = df_netflix.apply(
    lambda x: clean_prices(x["raw_price"]), axis="columns", result_type="expand"
)

Netflix's currency notation is not always standard. The Danish, Swedish, and Norwegian krone all appear as kr; R means Brazilian Real; Ft is Hungarian Forint; Rs is Pakistani Rupee. A mapping table resolves these into ISO codes, occasionally using the country name to disambiguate.

For exchange rates, the European Central Bank provides a daily CSV of EUR–currency rates. Using the EUR–USD rate, the code derives USD–currency rates and stores them in a lookup table. Several currencies used by Netflix — SAR, VND, PEN, NGN, COP, AED, KES — are not in the ECB database. For those, the analysis falls back to scraping XE.com and appends the resulting rates to the ECB table.

Finally, the Netflix price in US dollars for each country is computed as:

df_master["price_USD"] = df_master["clean_price"] / df_master["from_USD_to_X"]

Argentina is already a problem at this stage: due to hyperinflation and an unstable exchange rate, the converted price lands at an implausibly high value (over 250 USD per month). To avoid letting this artefact dominate the rest of the analysis, Argentina is excluded from all subsequent steps.

Step 3 — GDP per capita from the World Bank

The World Bank's API provides GDP per capita (in current US dollars) for each country:

url_wb = (
    "https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/all/indicator/"
    "NY.GDP.PCAP.CD?format=json&mrv=1&per_page=300"
)
json_wb = requests.get(url_wb).json()[1]

df_wb = pd.DataFrame(json_wb)[["countryiso3code", "value"]].rename(
    columns={"countryiso3code": "iso3", "value": "GDPPCAP"}
)

The geometry data (from UN WFP via Opendatasoft), the Netflix price data, the GDP per capita data, and the exchange rate table are then merged into a single GeoDataFrame keyed by country ISO codes. Countries and territories without Netflix are kept in the geometry but have missing price information, so they appear as light grey on the maps.

Step 4 — Relative Netflix prices

With prices in USD and GDP per capita in USD, a relative price is computed for each country:

[ p_{i,\text{rel}} = \frac{p_i^$}{y_i} ]

where (p_i^$) is the Netflix Standard-plan price in US dollars and (y_i) is GDP per capita for country (i).

This simple ratio already reveals that the absolute ranking ("who pays the most dollars?") and the relative ranking ("who sacrifices the largest share of income?") are not the same.

A scatter plot of price vs. GDP per capita makes this visible:

Two patterns stand out:

  • Netflix does charge more in richer countries: the regression line slopes upward.
  • But the slope is shallow. Income increases far faster than Netflix prices do.

In other words, richer countries get a better deal in relative terms: their higher subscriptions represent a much smaller share of average income than lower subscriptions in poorer countries.

Step 5 — Creating the Netflix index

The distribution of relative prices is strongly right-skewed: a small set of very poor countries with comparatively high Netflix prices produce a long tail of large ratio values.

To quantify this and make it comparable across countries, the analysis builds a Netflix index in three steps:

  1. Compute relative prices pi,relp_{i,\text{rel}}pi,rel​ as above.
  2. Apply a log transform.
  3. Remove extreme outliers and min–max scale the transformed values to a 0–100 range.

Skewness and outliers

Two countries stand out as extreme cases:

  • Argentina — already removed in Step 2 because the USD price is distorted by exchange-rate volatility.
  • Burundi — the lowest GDP per capita in the dataset combined with a $7.99 Netflix price yields a relative price far above the rest of the sample.

Burundi is therefore excluded from the index calculation as a relative-price outlier. All other countries remain.

Log transform and scaling

A log transform compresses the right tail while preserving the ordering of countries:

li=log⁡(pi,rel+1)l_i = \log(p_{i,\text{rel}} + 1) li​=log(pi,rel​+1)

The +1 ensures that all arguments to the logarithm are positive (some relative prices are small fractions). The transform makes the distribution more symmetric and prevents a few very large ratios from dominating the scale.

Finally, the log values are min–max scaled to a 0–100 index:

ni=li−min⁡jljmax⁡jlj−min⁡jlj×100n_i = \frac{l_i - \min_j l_j}{\max_j l_j - \min_j l_j} \times 100 ni​=maxj​lj​−minj​lj​li​−minj​lj​​×100

The resulting nin_ini​ is the Netflix index. It indicates how expensive a Netflix subscription is in country (i) relative to local GDP per capita and relative to all other countries in the sample:

  • Low values (close to 0) mean it is relatively cheap to subscribe given local income levels.
  • High values (close to 100) indicate a high subscription cost considering local standards of living.

In the final distribution, Monaco has an index of 0 (the cheapest subscription relative to income), while Afghanistan sits at 100 (the most expensive).

Step 6 — Visualising the results

Static maps

For a first look at the global pattern, two choropleth maps are produced using geopandas and matplotlib: one for absolute prices and one for the Netflix index. A small plotting function keeps the code compact:

def static_map(column, legend_label=None):
    fig, ax = plt.subplots()
    ax.set_axis_off()

    df.plot(
        ax=ax,
        column=column,
        cmap="OrRd",
        missing_kwds={"color": "lightgrey"},
        legend=True,
        legend_kwds={
            "orientation": "horizontal",
            "label": legend_label or column,
            "pad": 0.0,
            "aspect": 40,
            "shrink": 0.7,
        },
    )

    # remove the colorbar frame
    cbar_ax = fig.axes[-1]
    for spine in cbar_ax.spines.values():
        spine.set_visible(False)

    return fig, ax

The function is called twice, once for price_USD and once for netflix_index. The resulting maps are the same pair shown in the introduction; for reference, they are repeated here in a smaller side-by-side layout:

Absolute Netflix price (US$ per month) Netflix index (0–100)

  • In the Netflix index map, the top spots are occupied by Afghanistan, Central African Republic, Malawi, Madagascar, Somalia and other low-income countries.

Interactive Folium map

Static maps are useful for the overview, but an interactive map makes it easier to inspect individual countries in detail. The notebook builds a Folium map with a radio-button toggle between the absolute-price layer and the Netflix-index layer. Here, we can immediately see the drastic differences between absolute prices and the GDP-adjusted ones represented by the Netflix index.

Main findings

The contrast is clear: the countries that pay the most dollars per month are not the ones for whom Netflix is most expensive relative to income. The burden falls disproportionately on low-income countries. Summarising the numbers:

  • In absolute terms, the cheapest Standard-plan subscriptions are in Pakistan (about $2.84 per month), Nigeria (about $4.64), and Kenya (about $5.40).
  • The most expensive subscriptions in absolute terms are in Switzerland and Liechtenstein (around $29.37), followed by Denmark / Faroe Islands / Greenland (about $19.95) and the United States (about $17.99).
  • On the Netflix index, the highest relative prices appear in Afghanistan (index 100.0), Central African Republic (95.5), and Malawi (95.3), indicating that a Netflix subscription eats up a comparatively large share of average income.
  • The lowest relative prices are found in Monaco (index 0.0), Bermuda (5.9), and Macao (8.9), where a subscription is almost a rounding error at average income levels.

Caveats and limitations

A few things are worth keeping in mind:

  • Plan differences: Netflix's content library and plan structure vary by country (for example, ad-supported tiers in some markets). This analysis focuses on the Standard plan where available, but the offer is not perfectly identical.
  • Taxes: Prices are net of VAT or sales tax in some countries and gross in others. Netflix's own pricing pages are not fully consistent on this.
  • Exchange rate volatility: Exchange rates, especially for currencies like the Argentine Peso, can change dramatically between data collection and reading. Argentina was excluded as an outlier for this reason.
  • GDP per capita as a proxy: GDP per capita is a country-level average and says nothing about income distribution. A subscription that looks affordable on average may still be out of reach for many households.

Wrapping up

This project started from a simple observation — Netflix is cheaper in poor countries — and asked whether that is actually true once we look beyond nominal prices. The Netflix index suggests that it is not: after accounting for local purchasing power, subscribers in Sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia pay far more relative to their income than subscribers in Western Europe or North America.

The pipeline is fully reproducible: the notebook scrapes everything fresh from Netflix, the World Bank, and the ECB/XE. The geodata, Netflix prices, and computed index are also stored in the repository so that the analysis remains reproducible even if the upstream sources change.

A natural next step would be to look at how prices have evolved over time, or to build similar indices for other global streaming services.
